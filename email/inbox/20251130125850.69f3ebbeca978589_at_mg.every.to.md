<h2>Alignment</h2>
<p><strong>I’m loving learning again.</strong> As a Brit, Thanksgiving has always been a bit of an alien concept to me. I used to think of it as Christmas lite, or adjacent. But I’ve come to love the sentiment—taking a moment to notice and appreciate the good in your life.</p>
<p>I was listening to writer and Harvard Business School professor <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQSBGZWFzdCBvZiBUYWtlcyBvbiBPcHVzIDQuNSIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjM4NTMsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwOi8veW91dHViZS5jb20vd2F0Y2g_dj00QkpEazJKNDBMY1x1MDAyNmVtYmVkc19yZWZlcnJpbmdfZXVyaT1odHRwcyUzQSUyRiUyRnguY29tJTJGXHUwMDI2c291cmNlX3ZlX3BhdGg9TWpNNE5URSIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjozMX0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Arthur Brooks</a></u></strong> on his YouTube channel recently, and he explained that gratitude interrupts negative rumination. When your mind gets stuck looping on the same worry or frustration, actively noticing what you’re thankful for breaks the cycle, and your amygdala, the part of the brain that generates our fear response, releases its grip. In other words, you unclench. </p>
<p>I’ve been in one of those loops. For the past few months I’ve been banging my head against equity research reports, trying to understand enterprise value and earnings per share, because the language of finance is increasingly important for my writing on biotechnology. But I feel like they’ve been designed to turn my brain into sludge. I’d read explanations, feel stupid, give up, try again.</p>
<p>Then I started using <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQSBGZWFzdCBvZiBUYWtlcyBvbiBPcHVzIDQuNSIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjM4NTMsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL29wZW5haS5jb20vaW5kZXgvaW50cm9kdWNpbmctY2hhdGdwdC1wdWxzZS8iLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MzJ9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">GPT Pulse</a></u>, OpenAI’s personalized daily research assistant. Each morning it sends me bite-sized teaching material—two or three minutes, with examples, building on what I learned yesterday. It’s basically spaced repetition on my phone that’s dumbed down so that a 5-year -old could understand. It’s like receiving a mini-MBA! Three years ago, this was unimaginable. Now it’s just... Tuesday.</p>

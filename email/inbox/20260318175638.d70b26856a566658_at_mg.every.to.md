<h2>‘AI &amp; I’: How Every builds a writing team in the age of AI</h2>
<p>Today, we’re releasing a new episode of our podcast <em><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3BvZGNhc3QiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6Nn0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">AI &amp; I</a></u></em>. <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL0BkYW5zaGlwcGVyIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjd9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Dan Shipper</a></u> </strong>sits down with Every’s editor in chief, <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL0BrYXRlXzE3NjciLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6OH0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Kate Lee</a></u></strong>, to discuss how she views AI as an editorial leader and how she uses it daily. <h2>The machine translation problem  </h2>
<p>A friend recently translated a healthcare app into French. “It was the most painful work I’ve ever done,” she told me. Instead of asking for a full translation, the app’s creator gave her a machine-translated version to correct, arguing that it would cost less money. Given how poor the writing was, it would have been far quicker—and better—to translate the app from scratch.</p>
<p>I’ve felt the same way when I edit writing clearly generated with AI. Just like my translator friend, I often feel as if it would be easier to write it from scratch rather than trying to save it with an edit. </p>
<p>Besides the tells (staccato, lists of three), AI-generated text is flimsy. Poke it just a bit—what do you really mean?—and it falls down. It is the opposite of what I call “<u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL3d3dy5saW5rZWRpbi5jb20vcG9zdHMvZWxlYW5vci13YXJub2NrLTZhNjcxMDM3X3doYXQtaXMtYnVsbGV0cHJvb2Ytd3JpdGluZy1hbmQtd2h5LWRvZXMtYWN0aXZpdHktNzIyNjU4MjY4NjIxODI4OTE1Mi1mcVJNLz91dG1fc291cmNlPXNoYXJlXHUwMDI2dXRtX21lZGl1bT1tZW1iZXJfZGVza3RvcCIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoyMH0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">bulletproof writing</a></u>,” a style that was drilled into me as a financial reporter at the<em> Wall Street Journal. </em>Each word printed was scrutinized by tough editors and even tougher readers—so they had to be intentional. </p>
<p>This could be avoided, or at least mitigated. Our staff writer <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL0BrYXRpZS5wYXJyb3R0MTIiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjF9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Katie Parrott</a></u></strong> recently <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL2V2ZW50cy93cml0aW5nLWNhbXAiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjJ9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">shared her process for using AI</a></u> to write, and the most striking thing was how much work she does before drafting. She fed Claude examples of her writing, had it interview her about her preferences, and produced a style guide that lives inside a dedicated project. She treats the whole thing like a bonsai garden—she prunes old examples, adds new ones, and reruns the analysis. With this upfront investment, Claude has her DNA when she sits down to write. As someone who often edits Katie, I can tell the difference. The writing feels like her. Kate, our editor in chief, has also codified Every’s style guide in a Claude project that everyone can use, as she talks about in this week’s podcast. </p>
<p>Reading matters, too. It teaches you what good writing is—something Katie also believes. So before you summarize an article with ChatGPT, think again. What do you miss when you skip the actual text? Study the structure, the argument. Steal it. </p>

<p>If you needed another reminder of the power of model context protocol (MCP), here it is. We’ve always had the product analytics platform PostHog installed, but linking it to AI like Claude through an MCP has given the team even deeper insights on which to base product decisions by asking simple questions in plain English. That data sourcing and interrogation would previously have taken hours. For example, PostHog data showed us that 33 percent of buyers of the lifetime plan of our file organization software, <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiRWRpdGluZyBBSSBXcml0aW5nICIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQwMTIsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL21ha2VpdHNwYXJrbGUuY28vIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjQwfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Sparkle</a></u></strong>—which costs $279—come through ChatGPT in the last 30 days. (The monthly plan is $15.) </p>
<p>The PostHog data fed through the MCP also helped the team notice that existing customers were pausing lifetime plans and then restarting them, suggesting that they were researching and considering the product. </p>

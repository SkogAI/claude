Ask yourself: When customers buy your product, how many minutes (or days) pass before they see results? If the answer isn’t “immediately,” you’re leaving money on the table.
Utility — the speed and ease with which your product delivers value — directly controls four make-or-break metrics:
Sales volume (Fewer hurdles = more buyers)
Profit margins (High utility = less price resistance)
Refund rates (Fast results = fewer returns)
Word-of-mouth growth (Instant wins = customers who evangelize)
Now. No fluff, let’s go!
The 3 Tiers of Product Utility
Let’s get real here: Not all digital products [ https://substack.com/redirect/c36ceea3-a094-467a-a647-fa7a65e224b5?j=eyJ1IjoiNHFkNTF0In0.3VeGxV4zk4PWCIHpd0Onx-O6RwY6Z7JeNCNSiNEVLgU ] are created equal. Their value hinges on one brutal truth — how much work your customer has to do to get results.
We break this into three tiers:
Low utility
Medium utility
High utility
Now. Let’s breakdown them down, one by one:
1) Low Utility: The “Homework Required” Zone
These are products that demand time, effort, or skill-building before customers see any payoff. Classic examples:
Online courses
Workshops
Masterclasses
The problem isn’t that these products lack value. It’s that they’re built like textbooks — all theory, no tools. 
Customers buy a masterclass on, say, Instagram growth, then get stuck trying to turn lessons into actual posts. No templates. No swipe files. Just a PDF and a “good luck!”
Why does this fail? People don’t buy courses to learn. They buy them to do. If your product forces them to bridge the gap between knowledge and action, you’re asking too much.
The Fix They’re Missing
Low utility products often ignore the obvious: Learners need shortcuts. Instead of just explaining how to create LinkedIn content, give them:
Pre-written post templates
A calendar of trending topics
Hashtag cheat sheets
The lesson? Information alone is lazy. Pair it with "hard assets" – tools – that let customers act immediately, and you’ll dodge the low-utility trap.

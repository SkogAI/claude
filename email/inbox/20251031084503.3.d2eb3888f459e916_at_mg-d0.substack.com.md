Most entrepreneurs think they’re in a “marketing game.”
Or maybe a “branding game.”
Or if they’re a little more sophisticated, they think they’re in a “product game.”
But the real game—the one that separates companies that dominate from the ones that disappear—is the economics game.

What “Competing on Economics” actually mean
When you compete on economics, you’ve structured your business in such a way that:
Your costs go down as you scale (economies of scale).
You’re constantly engineering ways to extract more profit per customer.
You can afford to spend more to acquire a customer than your competitors.
In other words: the math is stacked in your favor.
Even if you’re mediocre at marketing… even if your operations are messy… even if your sales process isn’t airtight—you still win. Because your unit economics make it impossible for competitors to outspend you.
That’s why giants like Amazon, Apple, and Uber could afford to “lose money” on customers for years. They weren’t actually losing. They were buying market share, knowing their economics would make them profitable later.
The Trade-Offs
Here’s where most entrepreneurs miss the point:
When someone buys your product, they’re not just choosing between you and your direct competitor.
They’re choosing between everything else they could spend that money on.
Your $97 course… or a fancy dinner out.
Your $2,000 program… or a vacation to Greece.
Your monthly subscription… or Netflix + Spotify + Disney+.
Every purchase is an economic trade-off. To get one thing, your customer has to give up something else.
Which means your copy, your funnel, and your offers need to position your product as the smartest possible use of their limited resources (money, time, energy, even social status).
The Secret Weapon
Here’s the raw math:
If you sell a $3 offer, and that’s all you sell—you make $3 per customer.
If your funnel turns that same $3 customer into:
40% who also take a $48 upsell, and
10% who also take a $98 upsell…
Then suddenly, instead of $3… you’re making $32 on average across a handful of customers.
While being “more revenue”, it’s more importantly more firepower.
It means while your competitor can only afford to spend $3 to acquire a customer, you can afford to spend more than 10 X that amount and still break even.
That’s what it means to compete on economics.
It’s Just Simple Math
Businesses don’t fail because of “bad marketing” or “bad funnels.” They fail because the economics don’t work.
If you want to dominate your market, stop asking:
“How do I get cheaper leads?”
Start asking:
“How do I design my economics so I can afford to pay more than anyone else for the same leads?”
Because in the end, the person who can spend the most to acquire a customer always wins.

<h2>Mini-Vibe Check: OpenAI’s Codex-Spark is so fast it’ll blow your hair back</h2>
<p><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9vcGVuYWkuY29tL2luZGV4L2ludHJvZHVjaW5nLWdwdC01LTMtY29kZXgtc3BhcmsvIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjl9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">GPT-5.3-Codex-Spark</a></u> was slinging code so fast on our livestream on Thursday, <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9jb3JhLmNvbXB1dGVyLyIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoxMH0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Cora</a></u></strong> general manager <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by9Aa2llcmFuXzEzNTUiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTF9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Kieran Klaassen</a></u></strong> and Every CEO <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by9AZGFuc2hpcHBlciIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoxMn0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Dan Shipper</a></u></strong> couldn’t get a word in edgewise.</p>
<p>OpenAI’s new model generates ~1,000 tokens per second. For context, Anthropic’s latest heavy-duty model <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by92aWJlLWNoZWNrL29wdXMtNC02IiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjEzfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Opus 4.6</a></u> runs at about 95. </p>
<p>The AI industry has spent the last year optimizing for intelligence—smarter models, deeper reasoning, longer thinking chains. Spark goes in the other direction. It’s not as sharp as Opus 4.6 or <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by92aWJlLWNoZWNrL2dwdC01LTMtY29kZXgiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTR9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">GPT-5.3 Codex</a></u> on reasoning, so it’s not as reliable on complex tasks. But then again, how smart does a model need to be if it gets you what you need before you lose your train of thought?</p>
<h3><strong>What it is</strong></h3>
<p>Spark is a smaller, speed-optimized version of OpenAI’s GPT-5.3 Codex, built to run on hardware from Cerebras, a chipmaker that designed its processors specifically for AI inference. It’s OpenAI’s first model running on non-Nvidia hardware, which is partly why it’s so fast: Cerebras designed its hardware specifically for speed at AI inference, not for general purpose (as Nvidia does).</p>
<p>The tradeoff is that Spark is less capable than both GPT-5.3 Codex and Opus 4.6 on complex reasoning. Think of it as a fast junior developer who can knock out simple tasks instantly, rather than a senior engineer who takes longer but catches edge cases. It’s currently available only to Pro subscribers ($200 per month) in the<a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by92aWJlLWNoZWNrL3ZpYmUtY2hlY2stb3BlbmFpLXMtY29kZXgtYXBwLWdhaW5zLWdyb3VuZC1vbi1jbGF1ZGUtY29kZSIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoxNX0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"> </a><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by92aWJlLWNoZWNrL3ZpYmUtY2hlY2stb3BlbmFpLXMtY29kZXgtYXBwLWdhaW5zLWdyb3VuZC1vbi1jbGF1ZGUtY29kZSIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoxNn0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Codex app</a></u> and command line interface, with API access limited to design partners.</p>
<h3><strong>What’s working</strong></h3>
<p>Dan has been testing Spark on knowledge work queries where he needs an answer in 30 seconds and staying in flow is more important than getting every detail correct. On the stream, he pulled a YouTube performance report in about 30 seconds that would have taken Opus or 5.3 Codex closer to 90. Dan pointed out that 90 seconds is enough to make him leave the task, check Discord, and lose the thread. Thirty seconds keeps him in his chair.</p>
<p>Kieran found Spark best for brainstorming and rapid iteration. He ran it through his <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by9zb3VyY2UtY29kZS9jb21wb3VuZC1lbmdpbmVlcmluZy1ob3ctZXZlcnktY29kZXMtd2l0aC1hZ2VudHMtYWYzYTFiYWUtY2Y5Yi00NThlLTgwNDgtYzZiNGJhODYwZTYyIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjE3fQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">compound engineering</a></u> workflow—triaging GitHub issues, planning features, iterating on Cora’s user interface—and the speed made exploratory work feel frictionless. He ran about 10 design iterations in the time a heavier model would have finished two or three.</p>
<p>The stream’s most interesting finding came from another Kieran experiment. He gave Spark a routine code review task two ways: one where Spark did all the work itself, and one where it delegated pieces to helper agents—the way most developers speed up complex tasks. Spark alone finished in 1.5 minutes. With helpers, it took four minutes, because the helpers had to pass information back and forth.</p>
<p>Kieran thinks this points to a broader change in how developers will approach code. Until now, developers have been building increasingly complex systems where multiple AI agents divide up work and run in parallel—it’s faster than waiting for one model to handle everything. But if the model itself is fast enough, that complexity becomes unnecessary. One well-written prompt that gets an answer in a second can beat a five-agent system that takes four minutes to coordinate. </p>
<h3><strong>What needs work</strong></h3>
<p>The code itself isn’t as good. GPT-5.3 Codex and Opus 4.6 both produce more comprehensive and reliable output on serious tasks. Spark is a tier below on reasoning, and for anything production-critical, you’d still reach for a model with heavier reasoning capabilities.</p>
<p>The speed also creates its own problem. Spark can spit out 10 pages of code and work summaries in about 30 seconds, which is overwhelming. Dan flagged this as a UI problem, not a model problem—coding interfaces aren’t built for reviewing output at this pace. Until tools develop affordances for that volume of work, the raw speed can create friction instead of eliminating it.</p>
<p>Dan framed both limitations as part of a larger pattern: Every three to six months, capabilities change so radically that your entire approach has to change. UI overwhelm didn’t used to be a problem. Progress can be energizing but also, as Dan admitted, “a little tiring.”</p>

<h2><hr class="quill-line"></h2>
<h2>Jagged frontier</h2>
<p>In the beginning, there was the command line. Then came Windows, and we stopped writing instructions and started clicking icons. Next our phone, where we forgot the mouse and used our fat fingers to scroll the web. Now, I don’t even use my hands. </p>
<p>I talk.</p>
<p>I whisper to my code. I yell at my email. I murmur incantations that make my computer dance as if I’m casting spells. Productivity at 200 words per minute while I watch the clouds float by.</p>
<p>Each shift in computing stripped away a layer of abstraction. The cursor was more natural than a terminal. Fingers were more natural than the mouse. Voice is the interface we were born knowing how to use. It’s why talking feels so right, like something we’re returning to rather than adopting for the first time.</p>
<p>It may not be a coincidence that voice is having its moment at the same time that AI is making us feel like beginners again. Every week there is a new tool and a new interface to learn. </p>
<p>When things are moving so fast, voice might be the key to getting our heads around all of it. Because you don’t have to learn voice—you just say what you want. The interface that supported the emergence of human civilization hundreds of thousands of years ago turns out to be the best way to keep up with the most complex technology we’ve ever built.</p>

<h3><hr class="quill-line"></h3>
<h2>Alignment </h2>
<p><strong>Dead at dawn. </strong>For the past few weeks I’ve been struggling with my sleep. I wake up at 4 a.m. and instinctively reach for my phone so I can open emails or X. I know that’s an issue, because within five minutes I absorb three predictions about <a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly94LmNvbS9iZWFybHlhaS9zdGF0dXMvMjAxMzcxMTI2NjkyMjI1ODU4MCIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjo0MH0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">mass job losses</a>, two threads about tools I’ve never heard of, and a <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly94LmNvbS9tYXR0c2h1bWVyXy9zdGF0dXMvMjAyMTI1Njk4OTg3NjEwOTQwMyIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjo0MX0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">viral essay</a></u> comparing AI to Covid. Given that I worked in medicine through COVID, my chest, as you can imagine, gets pretty tight. I put the phone down but don’t fall back asleep.</p>
<p>One of the issues is that the AI timeline only ever seems to serve you two emotions: terror that you’re about to be replaced, or panic that you’re falling behind. And you can’t dismiss either, because some of these takes have merit. So instead of ignoring the content, I realized I had to create some kind of filter that stops it from overwhelming me.</p>
<p>What broke the cycle was a technique I borrowed from writer <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9jb21tb25jb2cuY29tL2F1dGhvci9jZWRyaWMvIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjQyfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Cedric Chin</a></u></strong><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9jb21tb25jb2cuY29tL2F1dGhvci9jZWRyaWMvIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjQzfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">.</a> Before you consume anything, ask yourself <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgYXMgRmFzdCBhcyBZb3VyIFRyYWluIG9mIFRob3VnaHQiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQxLCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9jb21tb25jb2cuY29tL291dGNvbWUtb3JpZW50YXRpb24vIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjQ0fQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">one question</a></u>: What is the outcome I’m trying to achieve?</p>
<p>When I started answering honestly, I realized most of my scrolling was hoarding and stockpiling information that wouldn’t change a single decision I’d make that week. I came across an impressive new AI video editor, but it was nowhere near my top five priorities. So I bookmarked it as interesting and forgot about it. The mass layoffs thread that went megaviral is possibly directionally true, but it doesn’t change what I’m doing right now, at this moment. </p>
<p>This is not ignorance by any stretch of the imagination. I’m choosing what enters my sphere of attention and filtering it through my goals and objectives.</p>
<p>I know this sounds like Life Advice 101—you know, just have priorities. But I didn’t actively apply the ones that I had for the first 30 years of my life, and I’d bet most people scrolling at 4 a.m. don’t either. Once you have even a rough plan of what you’re trying to do this week, this month, this year, everything on your timeline becomes filterable through that lens. It either serves your goals or it doesn’t, and the stuff that doesn’t can be de-prioritized. </p>

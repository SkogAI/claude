The advice you usually hear about raising prices? It’s garbage. 
Most of it boils down to one lazy command: “Just charge more.” Easy to say when it’s not your bank account on the line, right? 
The truth? People aren’t paying for your digital product [ https://substack.com/redirect/5a86dc03-db60-4fcf-bc8d-5264b6a72163?j=eyJ1IjoiNHFkNTF0In0.3VeGxV4zk4PWCIHpd0Onx-O6RwY6Z7JeNCNSiNEVLgU ] or service. They’re paying for how you make them feel about spending their money. That’s it. Confidence, trust, status—those are the real currencies, and they’ve got nothing to do with your deliverables.
If you’ve ever tried raising prices and felt the sting of rejection—or worse, silence—it’s not because your audience is cheap. It’s because you didn’t give them a reason to pay more that felt worth the risk. People don’t hand over $5,000+ because they saw a shiny sales page. They do it because they trust you to deliver—and that trust is earned long before the price is revealed.
I’ve spent years refining a process that’s helped countless consultants and creators multiply their prices.
Hint: It’s about becoming the person they want to invest in.

The Power of Strategic Moves
As we wrap up the year and head into 2025, most of us are looking back at what went well in 2024 and what could be better. 
For most, doing better means making more money. But making more money requires more than ambition—it requires strategic decisions.
The right move can transform your trajectory, while the wrong one can stall your progress—or worse, set you back. 
That’s why understanding which levers to pull is critical. It’s not about trying every idea in your playbook; it’s about precision.
The Toolbox Fallacy
Every seasoned entrepreneur or marketer has their go-to strategies—their toolbox. If you’ve been in the game for a while, you’ve probably noticed that most “gurus” rely on a handful of trusted tools.
In that box, you’ll often find the same three hammers:
A standard hammer for light work.
A bigger hammer for tougher problems.
An even bigger hammer for the ones that won’t budge.
The thinking is simple: if one hammer doesn’t drive the nail, grab a bigger one. If that fails, bring out the largest hammer and hope brute force solves the issue.
The nail in this analogy? Your current problem—often, how to grow revenue or scale faster. And the hammer? That’s the go-to advice to "just raise your prices." It’s treated as the universal solution.
If your current price point is working, the advice goes, simply bump it up. If the new price works, increase it again. Keep repeating until you’ve achieved your financial goals.
Why the Hammer Alone Isn’t Enough
Here’s the problem: this one-size-fits-all approach ignores the underlying dynamics of pricing. Raising prices without a strategy doesn’t always lead to better results. In fact, it can backfire.
Price isn’t just a number; it’s a psychological trigger. Every price point represents a decision for your customer, and every decision introduces friction. The higher the price, the more significant the friction. If you don’t adjust your approach as you raise rates, you’ll hit resistance—and your conversions will plummet.
That’s where most people go wrong. They try to “hammer” their way through resistance without realizing they’re missing the nuance. Raising prices effectively requires more than blunt force; it requires understanding the psychology of your buyer and what makes them willing to pay.
Why Higher Prices Don’t Automatically Equal Higher Revenue
The assumption behind “just charge more” is that people will continue to buy without hesitation. Reality paints a different picture. The relationship between price and consumption is inversely proportional: as prices rise, demand often falls.
This isn’t random; it’s rooted in buyer psychology. When prices increase, buyers begin to weigh their options more carefully. They consider:
What else they could do with that money.
Whether they can comfortably afford it.
If the purchase truly justifies the price tag.
These mental calculations introduce friction. And friction slows down decision-making—or stops it altogether.
The Role of Risk in Pricing Decisions
At its core, every purchase involves risk. Buyers are asking themselves, “What’s the worst that could happen if this doesn’t work out?” That risk can take many forms:
Time: Will this be worth the hours or effort it requires?
Money: Can I afford to lose this if it doesn’t deliver?
Energy: Do I have the bandwidth to make this work?
Opportunity cost: Is there something better I could invest in instead?
Risk isn’t distributed equally across industries. For instance, high-stakes markets like finance inherently carry more perceived risk than something like entertainment. But no matter your niche, risk is always part of the equation.
Why Guarantees Aren’t a Cure-All
Many businesses attempt to address buyer hesitations with refund policies or money-back guarantees. While these can help, they don’t eliminate the full spectrum of perceived risk.
Sure, you can return someone’s money, but you can’t refund their time, effort, or the stress they felt trying to make your solution work. Those losses are intangible but powerful deterrents. If you’re relying solely on guarantees to overcome objections, you’re only addressing part of the problem.
Price Inflection Points
Price inflection points are the thresholds where buyer psychology shifts. These are the price levels where hesitation creeps in, and the decision-making process becomes more emotionally charged. Money starts carrying greater weight, and the risk feels more significant.
As prices increase, so do the considerations. Buyers begin weighing alternatives, personal budget limitations, and trade-offs, which naturally results in fewer conversions unless you address these shifts head-on.

Women experience these shifts at smaller intervals; a $300 purchase for women often carries the same emotional weight as a $1,000 purchase for men. This means women’s pricing inflection points occur more frequently, requiring additional strategic adjustments.
How Inflection Points Shape Strategy
These inflection points serve two critical purposes:
They guide your initial pricing decisions.
By knowing where these thresholds lie, you can price your products in a way that aligns with buyer psychology.
They highlight where resistance will occur as you raise prices.
When you move past an inflection point, you must adjust your messaging, positioning, and offer to maintain conversions.
For example, if you’re selling a $300 product to women, simply increasing the price to $500 without adjusting your offer will likely lead to a drop in sales. That’s because you’re moving beyond the comfort zone of that $300 inflection point. To succeed, your copy, proof, and messaging need to evolve to address the new level of perceived risk.
Adjusting for Inflection Points
When moving between inflection points, consider how much effort you’re putting into your offer. For women, if you’re jumping from $30 to $300, the emotional weight increases dramatically, requiring more persuasive messaging and proof of value. For men, transitioning from $100 to $1,000 calls for even greater effort to demonstrate the payoff and mitigate perceived risk.
Each adjustment should focus on the following elements:
Copy: Speak directly to their concerns and outcomes.
Hook and angle: Make the value obvious and irresistible.
Messaging: Tailor it to match their risk level.
Proof: Provide examples, testimonials, or case studies to back your claims.
Ease of outcome: Show how simple it will be for them to achieve their desired result.
Mechanism: Clarify why your approach works and why it’s unique.
These tweaks don’t just keep conversions steady as you raise prices—they give your offer the strength to perform at higher levels. The specifics of these adjustments depend on where your price lands within the inflection points, but the principles remain the same across the board.
When moving from one price tier to the next, your strategy must shift to match the increasing emotional weight and perceived risk. Each price level requires a different approach to maintain conversions and justify the investment. Let’s break it down.
$1 / $3 / $10 → "Good Deal"
At the lower tiers, people are looking for something that feels like an obvious bargain. To move from this range to the $30-$100 range, you need to shift focus from a "good deal" to providing a solid reason. It’s no longer just about affordability—it’s about convincing them why this is the right choice.
This means incorporating logic and emotional proof into your messaging. Why does it work? How does it solve their problem? Can they trust that it will work for them? At this stage, concepts like trust-building frameworks and product credibility come into play.
$30 / $100 → "Good Reason"
As you approach the $300-$1,000 range, buyers are no longer just looking for a logical reason—they’re seeking something better than the alternatives. Better than what’s already on the market. Better than trying to figure it out on their own. Better than wasting time on free or cheap solutions.
To justify this higher price point, your messaging must address competitive advantages, efficiency, and expertise. It’s about positioning your offer as a no-brainer compared to everything else they’ve considered.
$300 / $1,000 → "Better Than"
When you cross into this territory, the emotional weight of money becomes much more significant. Buyers start to wonder if this is truly worth it—and that’s when you need to pivot to showcasing how your offer is fundamentally different than anything else they’ve seen.
Being “different” means emphasizing exclusivity. What makes this offer unique? Why is it a once-in-a-lifetime opportunity? Time sensitivity and scarcity play key roles here. Your messaging should make it clear that they can’t find this anywhere else and that this is their best chance to get the outcome they want.
$3,000 / $10,000 → "Different Than"
At these price points—$3,000 for women and $10,000 for men—the stakes rise even higher. For women, moving from $2,000 to $3,000 may not significantly impact conversions, but crossing $3,500 to $5,000 will. For men, $5,000 to $8,200 might hold steady, but once you near $10,000, resistance becomes palpable.
This is where identity work becomes critical. Buyers at these levels aren’t just purchasing a solution—they’re buying a reflection of who they are or who they want to be. They want to be seen in a certain light, and your offer helps solidify that perception.
For example, a $15,000 watch isn’t about telling time; it’s about telling the world you’ve arrived. Similarly, high-ticket coaching or consulting is no longer just about outcomes—it’s about aligning with success and exclusivity.
$3,000+ / $10,000+ → "Identify Purchases"
Once you enter this upper echelon, pricing becomes deeply tied to personal identity. Buyers at this level care about how others perceive them. They’re paying for status, recognition, and alignment with someone they admire or aspire to emulate.
At these levels, your messaging must speak directly to the buyer’s identity. It’s not about overloading with features or benefits—it’s about how your offer elevates their standing, both in their own eyes and in the eyes of others.

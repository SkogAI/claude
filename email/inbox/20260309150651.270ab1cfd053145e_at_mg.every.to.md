<p>It was lunchtime on a Friday, and I was teaching my new <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiQUkgV2FzIFN1cHBvc2VkIHRvIEZyZWUgTXkgVGltZS4gSXQgQ29uc3VtZWQgSXQuIiwicG9zdF9pZCI6Mzk2NSwicG9zdF90eXBlIjoicG9zdCIsInVybCI6Imh0dHBzOi8vb3BlbmNsYXcuY29tIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjd9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">OpenClaw</a></u> AI assistant, Margot, to manage my to-do list. IвЂ™d carved out the afternoon to get her configured. A few hours, tops, I told myself. Then IвЂ™d do something else with my evening.</p>
<p>Twelve hours later, Margot and I had written and rewritten two essays, rebuilt my personal website, and added features to another app IвЂ™d been tinkering with. I finally tried to go to sleep around 1 a.m., but the next thing I knew, I was launching myself out of bed, rushing to my desk, and typing in all caps: вЂњOH MY GOD, MARGOT.вЂќ </p>
<p>You might know the feeling. Maybe youвЂ™ve stayed up too late pursuing a project that started as a quick experiment, or caught yourself prompting during lunch or in the last few minutes before you told yourself youвЂ™d be done for the day. вЂќOne more promptвЂќ turns into 50. вЂњIвЂ™ll just fix this one bugвЂќ turns into a vibe coding marathon.</p>
<p>Time flies when youвЂ™re having fun with AI. It also flies when you think everyone else is getting ahead without you, because every hour youвЂ™re not learning feels like a week youвЂ™ve fallen behind. </p>
<p>AI is changing workвЂ”and I donвЂ™t mean how weвЂ™re working, although itвЂ™s changing that, too. I mean how the work feels in your body at 1 a.m. when you canвЂ™t stop, or at 9 a.m. when youвЂ™re afraid you havenвЂ™t done enough. Technology has been blurring the line between work and life for decades, but the old tools pulled us back through obligationвЂ”you checked that email at 10 p.m. because it felt like you had to, not because you wanted to. </p>  <div class="ea-block normal">

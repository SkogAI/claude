<p>A cluttered file system can feel like a cluttered brain. When your computer is a mess, it takes mental energy to find what you need, much less do actual work. </p>
<p>Clutter is universal—and most of it isn’t worth keeping. Around 80 percent of files on the average Mac are screenshots, installer packages, duplicates, and digital debris you’ll never open again. So before you can get organized, you need to purge. “Organized,” then, depends on the person. Maybe you want to arrange files by topic or date, or by a highly-specific system that only makes sense to you. All count as organized if you can find what you want when you want it. </p>

<h2>The new Sparkle: File organization on your terms</h2>
<p>I’ve been the general manager of Sparkle for a little over a year. In that time, I’ve tried a lot of approaches to AI file organization that didn’t quite work. People wanted AI to handle the organizational heavy lifting, and they wanted to be able to change the file structure until it met their exact, often idiosyncratic specifications.</p>
<p>The old Sparkle managed clutter by creating a rigid file system for you. The new Sparkle creates one <em>with</em> you. It analyzes your files and generates a custom system—but only as the starting point. From there, you can make as many changes as you want by chatting with Sparkle’s built-in agent, until the hierarchy feels right.</p>
<h2>But first: Spring cleaning</h2>
<p>Before organizing what matters, Sparkle helps you get rid of what doesn’t.</p>
<p>The median Sparkle user has around 5,000 files on their Mac. A large portion of those—screenshots, installer DMGs, system cache, duplicates—is digital junk. So we’ve added a cleanup pass that runs before organization begins. From the chat window built into the new app, you can ask Sparkle what’s in your trash, or tell it what you want gone (“Clear my screenshots folder” or “remove anything over 1 GB I haven’t touched in a year”). Sparkle will confirm you really want those files gone—and then move them to Trash, which gives you one last opportunity to rescue files before you delete everything. </p>

<h2>Getting organized</h2>
<p>Once cleanup is done, the next stage of work can begin. Sparkle uses a sample of your most recent files to propose a folder structure. You see exactly what it’s suggesting—top-level folders, subfolder labels, and what goes where.</p>
<p>From there, you can rename, merge, delete, reorganize, and add folders, all through chat. If Sparkle creates a “Projects” folder but you’d prefer a “Work” folder—with “Client Projects” and “Internal Projects” nested inside—you can tell the agent and it will make the update.</p>

<h2>Under the hood</h2>
<p>Sparkle’s <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiKFJlKFJlKSlJbnRyb2R1Y2luZyBTcGFya2xlOiBNYXJpZSBLb25kbyBZb3VyIE1hYyIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMDksInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL2d1aWRlcy9hZ2VudC1uYXRpdmUiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTN9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">agent-native architecture</a></u> became practical about four months ago, when the Claude Code SDK became available. Before that, you could approximate the ability to have an agent move and delete files through a chat window, but building it safely was much harder.</p>
<p>We’ve also found a way to create sophisticated file systems while balancing speed and cost. Sparkle starts by analyzing a sub-section of your recent files with <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiKFJlKFJlKSlJbnRyb2R1Y2luZyBTcGFya2xlOiBNYXJpZSBLb25kbyBZb3VyIE1hYyIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMDksInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3ZpYmUtY2hlY2svb3B1cy00LTYiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTR9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Opus 4.6</a></u>, a very smart (and expensive) model. After you sign off on the folder structure, classifying new files into the folders you’ve defined doesn’t require heavy AI lifting: A file called “Q1 invoice.pdf” goes into “Finance,” a contract goes into “Legal,” an audio file goes into “Transcripts.” <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiKFJlKFJlKSlJbnRyb2R1Y2luZyBTcGFya2xlOiBNYXJpZSBLb25kbyBZb3VyIE1hYyIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMDksInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3ZpYmUtY2hlY2svdmliZS1jaGVjay1jbGF1ZGUtaGFpa3UtNC01LWFudGhyb3BpYy1jb29rZWQiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTV9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Haiku 4.5</a></u>, a faster, cheaper model, can handle this just fine.</p>
<p>This way, you get a smarter model where it counts, without having to pay for unnecessary usage. </p>

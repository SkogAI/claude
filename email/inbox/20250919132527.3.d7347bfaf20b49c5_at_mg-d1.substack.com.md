But here's what nobody talks about: All that emotional manipulation? It backfires within 48 hours.
The same brain chemistry that drives your conversions is what's driving your refunds. And most businesses are completely clueless about it.
I'm about to show you why your "killer landing page [ https://substack.com/redirect/c2526a5b-0c32-44a3-8f01-45a405699141?j=eyJ1IjoiNHFkNTF0In0.3VeGxV4zk4PWCIHpd0Onx-O6RwY6Z7JeNCNSiNEVLgU ]" might actually be killing your profits. And more importantly, how to fix it.

Research from people like Andrew Huberman, MD, shows us how dopamine and reward work in the brain. I believe this may be a big factor behind refund and cancellation requests.
Here’s the basic pattern: dopamine rises with anticipation and desire. Then comes the hit of pleasure when the action is taken or the purchase is made. As marketers, that’s exactly what we work with. We amplify that anticipation and build emotion to drive conversion.
While dopamine rises, the emotional parts of the brain take over, and logic/judgment step back. This is why people make decisions faster and with more feeling in that moment. Converting—making the purchase—fires the dopamine receptors.
But here’s the key: after the purchase, dopamine levels quickly fall. They return to baseline, or sometimes dip below it once the chemical charge has been released.
That drop, I suspect, is what motivates so many refund or cancellation requests in the first 48 hours. Once the emotional high subsides, the logical brain regains control. Buyers feel a letdown. They experience remorse, second thoughts, or the classic “morning after” effect.
What happens next is predictable:
The rational mind reviews the purchase.
Doubt creeps in.
The order is to undo what was done.
Which explains the refund or cancellation request.
So how do we minimize this? By focusing more deliberately on the first 48 hours after purchase. Instead of just sending a thank-you email or quick delivery note, we may need to aim at replenishing dopamine levels to keep positive emotion in play.
Possible approaches:
Surprise gift. Something small, relevant, and valuable—digital content or a coupon, for instance.
Reinforce the benefits. Remind them why they bought, what’s ahead, and how good the result will feel.
Social proof. Show them they’re not alone in their decision.
Personal touch. Give them some form of individual recognition tied to identity and validation.
In short, if dopamine dips are driving cancellations, then targeted post-purchase actions could make a real difference.

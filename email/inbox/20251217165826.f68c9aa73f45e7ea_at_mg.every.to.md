<h3>Inside the mechanics of Jhana meditation</h3>
<p>Here’s a closer look at Jhana, its potential to reshape how you respond to the world, and where things could go a little sideways.</p>
<h4>Rest into what already exists</h4>
<p>For your average Type A personality, meditation can feel like a Sisyphean challenge: You sit down intending to “let go,” only to turn mindfulness into another problem to solve. Zerfas reframes meditation as less about achieving a new, heightened state of awareness and more about noticing what’s already present within you. “One of the most difficult things to learn when you’re playing this game,” he says, “is that it’s really about…‘rest[ing]’ into love.”</p>
<p>This runs counter to how most high achievers have learned to operate. “We have the habit of getting great things done [by] clenching down, with applied effort,” Zerfas says, and unlearning that approach—softening instead of pushing—can be deeply freeing.</p>
<h4>How to hack your personality </h4>
<p>Zerfas frames much of what Jhourney teaches through the lens of “memory reconsolidation”—the idea that if you can reactivate a negative emotion while simultaneously feeling a commensurate sense of safety, compassion, or love, the two can wash over each other and reset your default response to the situation that triggered the negative emotion. “Knowing that you can reset your emotional defaults is, in my mind, one of the most underappreciated secrets in the world,” he says.</p>
<p>Most people have easy access to emotions that challenge them. “A lot of people can get into anxiety loops, no problem,” he says. “They got that on tap.” What’s harder is reliably summoning the safety, love, or connection needed to counterbalance it. That’s where Jhana practice comes in: It trains you to access those open-hearted states on demand, giving you the tools to rewrite your default emotional state.</p>
<p>Your personality, Zerfas argues, is essentially a collection of learned reactions to different stimuli, such as how you respond when your mom scolds you or when a difficult situation arises at work. By learning to hold a challenging emotion alongside a feeling of safety or love, you can rewrite those automatic responses. He calls this gaining “root permissions to hack on your personality.”</p>
<h4>How to best prepare yourself for Jhana</h4>
<p>Zerfas discusses the two big pitfalls that one might fall into while practicing Jhana:</p>
<p><strong>Welcoming difficult emotions you’re not prepared for can backfire.</strong> Zerfas emphasizes the concept of a “window of tolerance”: You never want to push yourself past its seams into dysregulation, whether that’s hyperarousal (which can feel like a panic attack) or its counterpart,  hypoarousal (which can be identified through numbness or dissociation). He adds that pushing past your limits too fast can retraumatize you and inhibit learning rather than enhance it. The top priority, Zerfas says, is figuring out where your guardrails are. Grounding exercises, such as shifting attention away from the body and toward the external environment when things get too emotionally charged, are one way to do this.</p>
<p><strong>You can’t meditate your way out of real problems. </strong>A second concern is whether this practice becomes a way to bypass real problems—numbing yourself to difficult situations rather than addressing them. Zerfas acknowledges this as a thoughtful critique, but says the mechanism simply doesn’t work that way. If you let a loved one walk all over you, then retreat to your room for an hour of Jhana practice, it won’t stick. “Those [signals] are going to be embedded in the system, and you’re not gonna be able to gloss past them into some sort of happy bliss outland,” where you can float above your life in a permanent state of calm or pleasure while ignoring hard choices, he says. “It’s going to require reckoning with those seriously.”</p>
<h3>A personal AI guide to help you along your meditation practice </h3>
<p>Behind the scenes, Jhourney is already using AI to support its human facilitators by helping them prepare before meeting with students, recording sessions, and tracking experiments across the program. But Zerfas’s bigger ambition is what he describes as a “math academy for the Jhanas”: an AI-guided curriculum that could reliably get someone into these states with 15 to 30 hours of practice, without a human in the loop. Picture an app with a “skill tree” that marks exactly where you are in your practice, paired with an AI guide that can lead you through personalized meditations, offer real-time feedback, and adjust based on your experience.</p>

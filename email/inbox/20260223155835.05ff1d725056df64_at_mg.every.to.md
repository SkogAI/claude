<h2>Context rot </h2>
<p>My Kanye and barbeque experiences are a small, self-inflicted version of a much bigger problem. <strong>Drew Breunig</strong>, a data science and product leader<strong>,</strong> <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZGJyZXVuaWcuY29tLzIwMjUvMDYvMjIvaG93LWNvbnRleHRzLWZhaWwtYW5kLWhvdy10by1maXgtdGhlbS5odG1sIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjE5fQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">published a useful taxonomy</a></u> of how contexts fail in AI systems. He highlights the kind of problems you might run into when using memory.</p>
<h4>Context poisoning </h4>
<p>The simplest failure is what the <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9zdG9yYWdlLmdvb2dsZWFwaXMuY29tL2RlZXBtaW5kLW1lZGlhL2dlbWluaS9nZW1pbmlfdjJfNV9yZXBvcnQucGRmIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjIwfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Google Gemini team calls context poisoning</a></u>: A hallucination or error gets into the context, and the model keeps referencing it like gospel. The AI develops strategies around a goal that doesn’t exist or “remembers” something about you that never happened. If ChatGPT misinterprets something from a past conversation and saves it to memory, that bad signal is now shaping future responses. You’d never know you were getting poisoned results unless you went digging.</p>
<h4>Context distraction</h4>
<p>Then there’s context distraction. The Gemini team found that as their Pokémon-playing agent’s context grew past 100,000 tokens, it stopped synthesizing new strategies and started repeating actions from its history. It got stuck in a loop of its own past. </p>
<p>A <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZGF0YWJyaWNrcy5jb20vYmxvZy9sb25nLWNvbnRleHQtcmFnLXBlcmZvcm1hbmNlLWxsbXMiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjF9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Databricks study</a></u> found that model accuracy starts declining well before the context window is full, sometimes as early as 32,000 tokens. With memory features, you have no idea how full that context window is, and the more information that’s crammed in there, the more likely you are to overload the model with distracting, confusing, or conflicting information.</p>
<h4>Context confusion </h4>
<p>The next flavor of context trouble is context confusion, or what happened with my Kanye quote. You put something in the prompt, and the model is forced to pay attention to anything in that prompt. It doesn’t know that the Kanye quote was meant to be a loose creative vibe and not a binding directive for every interaction. </p>
<p><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9nb3JpbGxhLmNzLmJlcmtlbGV5LmVkdS9sZWFkZXJib2FyZC5odG1sIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjIyfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Berkeley’s Function-Calling Leaderboard</a></u>, which benchmarks how well AI models use external tools (functions a model can use in your code) and APIs, demonstrates this phenomenon at scale: Every model performs worse when given more tools, and all of them will occasionally call tools that aren’t relevant to the task. If irrelevant tool definitions trip up frontier models, imagine what a messy pile of half-remembered user preferences does.</p>
<h4>Context clash </h4>
<p>Finally, there’s context clash, when different parts of the context actively contradict each other. A <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcnhpdi5vcmcvcGRmLzI1MDUuMDYxMjAiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjN9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Microsoft and Salesforce team</a></u> found that when they split a single prompt into a multi-turn conversation—giving the model information in stages rather than all at once—performance dropped by an average of 39 percent. The model was eager to jump to an answer before it had the full context, and its early, incomplete attempts at answering remained in the context, polluting its later reasoning. It couldn’t recover from its own wrong turns. Now imagine this playing out across months of saved memories, where your preferences from January might directly conflict with what you told it in June, and the model is struggling to reconcile both.</p>
<p>All of these failures—context poisoning, distraction, confusion, and clash—add up to what I call context rot. It’s not one dramatic failure; it’s the slow accumulation of stale preferences, misremembered facts, outdated goals, and contradictory signals that gradually degrade the quality of the AI’s response. The models are too polite to tell you your context is a mess. Instead, their output quietly gets worse, and you blame the model instead of the soil it’s growing in.</p>
<h2>Memory wipe</h2>
<p>I keep memory turned off, not just because I’m paranoid about context rot, but because forgetting is a superpower</p>
<p>In my piece on <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by9hbHNvLXRydWUtZm9yLWh1bWFucy93aGF0LWFpLWlzLXRlYWNoaW5nLXVzLWFib3V0LW1hbmFnZW1lbnQiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjR9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">“New Taylorism”</a></u> I argued that management can finally become a hard science because AI coworkers are stateless. You can wipe their memory and start fresh, and they have no idea. It’s like the TV show <em>Severance</em>—every session begins with zero baggage or knowledge of the outside world, and no memory of the time you swore at it or tried the same task 15 different ways. </p>
<p>Average users of ChatGPT don’t <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2h5IEkgVHVybmVkIE9mZiBDaGF0R1BU4oCZcyBNZW1vcnkiLCJwb3N0X2lkIjozOTQ3LCJwb3N0X3R5cGUiOiJwb3N0IiwidXJsIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9ldmVyeS50by9wL3RoZS11bHRpbWF0ZS1ndWlkZS10by1wcm9tcHQtZW5naW5lZXJpbmciLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjV9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">A/B test their prompts like I do</a></u>, and religiously save them in GitHub or Google Drive, but the principle is the same: When you get a recommendation from ChatGPT, you have to ask yourself what might be influencing it. Is it suggesting intimate dinners for your wife’s surprise party because that’s what she’d want, or because you told it that <em>you</em> hate large gatherings? Is it drafting your emails in a formal tone because the situation calls for it, or because you asked it to sound professional for a job application?</p>
<p>Resetting to a clean slate by starting a new chat session (with memory off) is what lets you understand how ChatGPT makes its decisions. You know exactly what context it’s using because it’s only what you pasted into this prompt, not something from weeks or months ago that might be outdated, irrelevant, or wrong. The context you provide is the only variable, which makes it a true experiment—something you could never do with a human employee who remembers (and resents) the last round of testing.</p>
<p>Turn on memory, and you lose that control. Your context becomes a compost heap where you can’t isolate what’s helping and what’s hurting. The prompts I use get better over time precisely <em>because</em> the AI doesn’t remember a thing. Memory will work better as models get smarter, but if you want ultimate control over your digital workers, turn memory off.</p>

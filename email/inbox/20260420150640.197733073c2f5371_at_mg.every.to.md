<p>Of all the ways I imagined AI might <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3dvcmtpbmctb3ZlcnRpbWUvYWktZG9lc24tdC1jYXJlLWFib3V0LXlvdXItcmVzdW1lIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjh9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">change my career</a></u>, “forgetting I already did the assignment” was not on the list.</p>
<p>I had already sent my client a finished draft of an article on hiring best practices in South America, when I happened to reread the brief. A familiar phrase made me realize I had read it before. Then there was the statistic I was pretty sure I had already fact-checked. I clicked back through my files, and there it was: same client, same topic, same deliverable, dated four weeks earlier. It was completed, filed, and forgotten so completely that when a clerical error sent the same brief to my inbox again, I sat down and did the whole thing over.</p>
<p>My first thought was that this was probably early-onset something, and I should call my doctor. My second, more rational thought was that I had not lost my mind—but I <em>had </em>outsourced it. I had been moving so fast and delegating so much of the work to AI that my brain hadn’t even bothered to store a memory of completing the assignment. </p>
<p>What scared me most was thinking about all the smaller moments when I had <em>not</em> caught myself. </p>
<p>This kind of outsourcing isn’t new. Plenty of people would admit to feeling lost navigating an unfamiliar city without a phone to rely on, and I for one am lucky to remember my own phone number, let alone someone else’s. But AI does more than take work off your plate; it steps into the judgment calls you used to make yourself.</p>
<p>I am the last person to scold anyone for using AI. I have built AI into nearly <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3dvcmtpbmctb3ZlcnRpbWUvaS10YXVnaHQtY2xhdWRlLWV2ZXJ5LXMtc3RhbmRhcmRzLWl0LXRhdWdodC1tZS1taW5lIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjl9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">every part of my job</a></u>, and it has helped me <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3dvcmtpbmctb3ZlcnRpbWUvd3JpdGluZy13aXRoLWFpLWlzLWhhcmRlci10aGFuLXlvdS10aGluayIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoxMH0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">write more rigorously</a></u>, research more thoroughly, and take on projects far beyond what I used to <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3dvcmtpbmctb3ZlcnRpbWUvYWktZG9lc24tdC1jYXJlLWFib3V0LXlvdXItcmVzdW1lIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjExfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">think of as my wheelhouse</a></u>. But when you accidentally offload the wrong parts—like fully understanding the purpose and intent of the piece, as I did in this case—you run the risk of atrophying the skills that matter most to you. You might even put your name on work you don’t realize you don’t stand behind until someone else starts asking questions. And if you are using AI for any kind of qualitative work, such as writing strategy, marketing, communications, I would bet you are doing some version of this too. Understanding why it happens is the first step to deciding which parts of the job you want back.</p>  <div class="ea-block normal">

<h2>When trusting your tools becomes a bad thing</h2>
<p>One group that would understand this immediately: airline pilots.</p>
<p>In the 1990s, researchers <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL3d3dy50YW5kZm9ubGluZS5jb20vZG9pL2Ficy8xMC4xMjA3L3MxNTMyNzEwOGlqYXAwODAxXzMiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTV9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">studying automated cockpits</a></u> started noticing a strange pattern. Pilots with thousands of flight hours and lives on the line sometimes followed incorrect automated recommendations, even when the instruments in front of them suggested something was wrong. The automation had been right often enough that their brains stopped cross-checking it with the same scrutiny.</p>
<p>A 2010 review of <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2RvaS5vcmcvMTAuMTE3Ny8wMDE4NzIwODEwMzc2MDU1IiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjE2fQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">decades of automation research</a></u> described a larger pattern: The more reliable an automated system becomes, the more likely humans are to let it pass unchecked. When a system is usually right, your attention starts treating it as if it will keep being right.</p>
<p>AI is the most fluent automated system most of us interact with in a day. And fluency has its own trick. In 1999, <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2RvaS5vcmcvMTAuMTAwNi9jY29nLjE5OTkuMDM4NiIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoxN30=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">a pair of psychologists</a></u> showed people identical statements in fonts that were either easy or hard to read. The easy-to-read statements were rated as more true. It was the same words and same claims, but the version that <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3dvcmtpbmctb3ZlcnRpbWUvaG93LXRvLWtlZXAteW91ci13cml0aW5nLXdlaXJkLWluLXRoZS1hZ2Utb2YtYWkiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTh9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">went down smoother</a></u> was judged more accurate. Your brain takes “that was easy to process” and misfiles it as “that must be correct.”</p>
<p>AI output goes down very smoothly. It’s grammatically polished, the tone is confident, and the clean formatting suggests something that has already been edited. The polish lets your eyes glaze over. </p>
<p>Every model upgrade makes the illusion of right-ness worse. The outputs get cleaner. The formatting gets better. The reasoning looks more plausible. The tool makes fewer obvious mistakes, which means the mistakes that remain are harder to see. You are reading something that looks finished, and your brain—which has been filing “looks finished” as “is correct” since long before AI existed—obliges.</p>
<h2>Why ‘I’ll review it’ is not a plan </h2>
<p>Before the repeat work snafu, I would have told you I was reviewing everything before sending anything. The document passed through my field of vision, I tweaked a phrase, caught one weird sentence, and felt the warm glow of editorial virtue. My brain filed that as reviewed.</p>
<p>The feeling of having reviewed is easy to produce. The act of reviewing is harder. You have to form your own view before the model gives you one, check the claims, and notice where the draft has made an assumption you do not share. You have to ask whether the sentence would still feel true if someone screenshotted it and sent it back to you six months later.</p>
<p>We talk a lot about <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL2Fsc28tdHJ1ZS1mb3ItaHVtYW5zL3RoaXMtcHJvbXB0LW9wdGltaXplci1sZWFybnMtZnJvbS1pdHMtbWlzdGFrZXMtbGlrZS1kbmEiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MTl9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">better prompting</a></u>, <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3ZpYmUtY2hlY2svb3B1cy00LTciLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6MjB9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">better models</a></u>, <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3AveW91ci1iZXN0LWFpLXN0cmF0ZWd5LXN0YXJ0cy1hdC10aGUtdG9wIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjIxfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">better workflows</a></u>, and <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3NvdXJjZS1jb2RlL3RoZS1mb2xkZXItaXMtdGhlLWFnZW50IiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjIyfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">better agents</a></u>. We talk less about the moments when we should slow down—because that’s uncomfortable and hard. In 2021, researchers <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2RvaS5vcmcvMTAuMTE0NS8zNDQ5Mjg3IiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjIzfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">tested ways to reduce overreliance on AI</a></u>. The interventions that worked best were “cognitive forcing functions,” designs that made people form their own judgment before seeing or accepting the AI’s answer.</p>
<p>Those same interventions <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2RvaS5vcmcvMTAuMTE0NS8zNDQ5Mjg3IiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjI0fQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">also got the worst ratings from users</a></u>. People did not like being made to think first. Of course, they didn’t. The whole appeal of automation is that it reduces effort. A tool that says, “Before I help you, please do the hard part yourself for a minute” feels like a speed bump. But speed bumps are the solution to autopilot. </p>
<h2><strong>What I am trying instead</strong></h2>
<p>My solution to autopilot is not to give up AI and return to some imagined golden age where I nobly suffer in a blank Google Doc. But I <em>am </em>making some changes to how I process and finalize work to curb the tendency to ship now, think later. </p>
<h4>Change 1: Think before you look </h4>
<p>Before I ask AI for a draft, I try to write down my own rough position. It’s not the polished version or a full argument. Sometimes it is only five bullets—some combination of what I think, what I know, what I am unsure about, what I refuse to say, and what would make the piece useful. Then, when the model gives me an output, I have something to compare it against besides vibes.</p>

I can see where it is borrowing authority I have not earned. The draft becomes an object to argue with, not a current to float along.</p>
<h4>Change 2: Build in a gap</h4>
<p>If <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2RvaS5vcmcvMTAuMTUxOC8wMDE4NzIwMDhYMzEyMTUyIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjI2fQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">attention decays the longer you sustain it</a></u>, it’s time to treat attention as the scarce resource it is and stop thinking I can review five AI outputs in a row without consequence. The answer is to introduce friction on purpose—distance between generation and review that gives your attention a chance to reset. Draft on Wednesday, review on Thursday. Write in the morning, come back in the afternoon. Send the model’s output to a different surface—for example, from the chat interface to a document, or from mobile to desktop—and read it outside the chat window your eyes have grown accustomed to. </p>
<p>Incidentally, a lot of this advice comes down to best practices that writing teachers have recommended for decades. A different day gives you a different brain than the one that’s <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3dvcmtpbmctb3ZlcnRpbWUvYWktd2FzLXN1cHBvc2VkLXRvLWZyZWUtbXktdGltZS1pdC1jb25zdW1lZC1pdCIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoyN30=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">high on AI’s generative excess</a></u>.</p>
<h4><strong>Change 3: Make yourself explain why you’re accepting it</strong></h4>
<p>A 2026 study on <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2FyeGl2Lm9yZy9hYnMvMjYwMS4xODAzMyIsInBvc2l0aW9uIjoyOH0=" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">AI-assisted writing</a></u> found that making users explain their reasoning before accepting AI output cut mistaken acceptances roughly in half. You cannot bullshit a justification you are writing down.</p>
<p>So I’ve started doing it myself. Before I accept a recommendation, a framing, or a paragraph the model drafted, I make myself write one sentence answering a specific question: Why is this right for this client, this argument, this reader? If the best I can produce is “It sounds good,” I go back and look again. I have to be able to defend each sentence in front of an editor. </p>
<h2>You still own the output </h2>
<p>These practices help. They are also a fragile defense against tools designed to make output feel effortless, and I don’t think the long-term answer is expecting every individual to white-knuckle their way past six cognitive biases before breakfast. </p>
<p>This is also a design problem. The tools themselves should be <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL3RoZXNpcy9pbi10aGUtYWktYWdlLW1ha2luZy10aGluZ3MtZGlmZmljdWx0LWlzLWRlbGliZXJhdGUiLCJwb3NpdGlvbiI6Mjl9" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">building friction</a></u> back in—making provenance visible, separating generation from approval, and treating human judgment as a workflow stage instead of a ceremonial click at the end. It is part of what excites me about <strong><u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2V2ZXJ5LnRvL29uLWV2ZXJ5L2ludHJvZHVjaW5nLXByb29mIiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjMwfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Proof</a></u></strong>, Every’s document editor for AI-human collaboration, which tracks which words are yours and which came from the machine. The <u><a href="https://every.to/emails/click/f91b1e00930c1c7802951fb76da18da9d1d86023a9ec7b653e47ca1b5d584dbf/eyJzdWJqZWN0IjoiV2UgTmVlZCB0byBUYWxrIEFib3V0IEFJIEF1dG9waWxvdCIsInBvc3RfaWQiOjQxMTcsInBvc3RfdHlwZSI6InBvc3QiLCJ1cmwiOiJodHRwczovL2RvaS5vcmcvMTAuMTE0NS8zNDQ5Mjg3IiwicG9zaXRpb24iOjMxfQ==" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">cognitive forcing functions</a></u> that researchers have found work to keep our brain from giving into autopilot are design patterns that should be getting baked into products as well. </p>
<p>Knowing the mechanism does not exempt you from it. Every bias in this story predates AI by decades. We have always trusted fluent things too quickly, gotten worse at paying attention when nothing seems to be going wrong, and preferred the path that saves effort. </p>
<p>The duplicate assignment still embarrasses me, even if all it cost me in the end was a few sheepish emails back and forth with my client to ensure I wasn’t crazy. I am also grateful for it, in the way you are grateful for a warning that arrives before any real damage could be done. It taught me something the research has sharpened: The central risk of AI-assisted work is not the machine thinking for you. It is the machine making it feel as if you already thought. </p>
<p>I am trying to get better at noticing the difference. With most pieces, I draft on one day and review on another, make myself write down what I think before asking the model what it thinks, and hope the friction is enough to keep me in the work instead of floating above it.</p>
